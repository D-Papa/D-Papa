Who is Alan Turing?

Alan Mathison Turing was a British mathematician, logician, cryptographer and philosopher. He was considered one of the fathers of computer science and one of the greatest mathematicians of the 20th century. Turing worked at Bletchley Park, the UK's leading cryptanalysis center, where he devised a series of techniques for breaking German ciphers and built an electromechanical machine capable of decoding codes created by the Enigma cryptographic machine. Alan Turing's activity in the Bletchley Park group was covered by an absolute secrecy. At the end of the Second World War the British government imposed on those who had worked at encryption to break German, Japanese and Italian cryptographic codes, the prohibition of speaking or writing about any subject dealt with in that period. This prevented that Turing and his colleagues received the accolades that would have been extensively theirs elsewhere e publicly recognized. Information on these activities began to be published, by the British secret services, in 1974, when Turing and many of his colleagues were long dead.

The imitation game (Plot)

(The Imitation Game is the film adaptation of Alan Turing's 1983 biography.)

The Imitation Game is the film adaptation of A's 1983 biography In the midst of World War II, mathematician Alan Turing decides to put his skills at the service of the British government. Their goal is to end the war as soon as possible. Alan Turing shows up for the interview and convinces Commander Alastair Denniston to hire him despite not knowing German, but the first disagreements begin to appear when he realizes that he will not have to work alone, but with other brilliant mathematical minds. Turing begins to be disliked by his co-workers and boss Hugh Alexander because of his character. Deciphering the encoded codes of the Enigma machine is a mission deemed impossible by anyone, especially by the British authorities, as it is an extremely complex device, which has an enormous amount of possible message encryption keys, and the Germans change the encryption key every 24 hours, at the stroke of midnight every day. Turing decides to change his method, no longer acting in defense but in counterattack by creating a machine that automatically decrypts every single message.
